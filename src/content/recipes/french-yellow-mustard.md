A bright-yellow American-style prepared mustard engineered as a **by-weight
formula** so it scales perfectly linearly: dial in the flavor on a one-cup pilot
batch, then multiply every number by the same factor to make up to a full
gallon. The base is a clean copy of the supermarket classics — the
**French's-style** variant layers in paprika and garlic powder for that ballpark
flavor, while the **Annie's-style** variant keeps it to the clean, organic core
of seed, vinegar, turmeric, and salt.

## Technical overview

- **Active time:** 10 minutes
- **Rest time:** 24-48 hours (essential), up to 2 weeks for full mellowing
- **Flavor profile:** Sharp vinegar tang, mild mustard heat that fades over the
  first day, earthy turmeric, balanced salt.
- **Yield (pilot):** ~280 g, a little over 1 cup.

Yellow mustard's character is enzymatic, not just a matter of mixing. Whole
yellow seed (_Sinapis alba_) is flavorless; grinding and **hydrating the powder
with cold water** lets the myrosinase enzyme act on sinalbin to generate the
mustard's bite. Adding the vinegar too early — before the enzyme has worked —
locks in a harsh, raw bitterness, so we hydrate first and acidify second.

## The formula (by weight)

Everything is anchored to weight, so any batch is just the pilot column times a
multiplier. Mustard finishes at roughly the same volume it starts (~1.03 g/mL),
so the base batch sizes below are close to their finished volumes.

| Ingredient            | % of batch | Pilot (~1 cup) | Quart (3.4×) | Gallon (14×) |
| --------------------- | ---------- | -------------- | ------------ | ------------ |
| Yellow mustard powder | 21.4%      | 60 g           | 204 g        | 840 g        |
| Water (cold)          | 42.9%      | 120 g          | 408 g        | 1680 g       |
| Vinegar               | 32.1%      | 90 g           | 306 g        | 1260 g       |
| Salt                  | 2.1%       | 6 g            | 20 g         | 84 g         |
| Ground turmeric       | 1.4%       | 4 g            | 14 g         | 56 g         |
| **Base total**        | **100%**   | **~280 g**     | **~950 g**   | **~3920 g**  |

For the **French's-style** variant, add per batch: paprika at 0.7% of the base
(2 g pilot → 28 g gallon) and garlic powder at 0.5% (1.5 g pilot → 21 g gallon).

A digital scale that reads to 1 g is enough for the pilot batch; for the salt and
turmeric at small scale, a 0.1 g jeweler's scale makes the ratios exact. Once you
trust the formula, weighing straight into the blender jar is faster and far more
repeatable than measuring spoons.

## Execution

### 1. Hydrate the mustard powder

Whisk the **mustard powder** together with the **cold water** until you have a
smooth, lump-free paste. Let it stand **10-15 minutes**. This rest is where the
heat is born: the enzyme needs water and time before acid arrives. The paste will
smell sharp and sinus-clearing at this stage — that is correct, and it will calm
down dramatically over the next day.

### 2. Acidify and season

Whisk in the **vinegar**, **salt**, and **turmeric** (plus **paprika** and
**garlic powder** for French's-style). The acid halts enzyme activity and sets
the final heat level — the longer you waited in step 1, the more pungent the
result. The mixture will look thin and grainy; it thickens as the seed coat
hydrates.

### 3. Smooth it out

For supermarket-smooth texture, blend with an immersion or countertop blender for
1-2 minutes until creamy and pourable. For a coarser, grainier mustard, skip the
blender. The mustard thickens noticeably as it sits, so leave it slightly looser
than your target.

### 4. Rest to mellow

Transfer to a clean jar, cover, and refrigerate at least **24-48 hours** before
tasting. Fresh mustard is aggressively bitter and harsh; the rest rounds it into
the familiar mellow tang. Flavor keeps improving for up to two weeks.

## Flavor overlays

- **Honey Mustard** — whisk in honey (45 g pilot, ~16% of the base) after
  blending for a sweet-tangy condiment.
- **Smoky** — bloom 2 g of smoked paprika into the batch for a deeper, barbecue-
  leaning profile. Stacks with the French's-style variant.

## Scaling to a gallon

The formula is linear, so scaling is pure multiplication — but a few practical
notes for the big batch:

- **Mix dry into the full water volume first.** At a gallon you are hydrating
  ~840 g of powder; add it to the water gradually while blending to avoid a
  paste-bomb of dry clumps.
- **Hold the hydration rest.** The 10-15 minute enzyme rest matters at every
  scale — don't rush it just because the batch is big.
- **Blend in stages** if your blender jar can't take the full volume, then
  recombine.
- **Yield check:** the gallon base totals ~3.92 kg, which is ~1 US gallon of
  finished mustard once blended.

## Troubleshooting

- **Too bitter / too harsh.** It's underaged. Bitterness is normal on day zero
  and fades with the 24-48 hour rest; give it time before adjusting.
- **Not hot enough.** Hydrate longer with colder water before adding vinegar, and
  use less time-aged mustard — pungency naturally decays as the mustard ages.
- **Too thick.** Whisk in water a few grams at a time. It always thickens in the
  jar, so correct after the first day's rest.
- **Too thin.** Let it sit longer; the seed coat keeps absorbing liquid. For an
  immediate fix, blend in a little more mustard powder.
- **Color looks dull.** Turmeric brightens as it hydrates and as the pH drops
  with the vinegar; the full color sets in after resting.
